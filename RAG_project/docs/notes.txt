================================================================================
                     CATEGORIES OF SIGNALS - A COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE
================================================================================

A signal is any physical quantity that carries information and varies with time,
space, or another independent variable. Signals are fundamental to communication,
engineering, biology, finance, and many other fields.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. BASED ON TIME (CONTINUOUS vs. DISCRETE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Continuous-Time (Analog) Signals
  - Defined for every instant of time along a continuous time axis.
  - Examples: sound waves, temperature readings from a thermometer, ECG waveforms.
  - Represented mathematically as x(t), where t is a real-valued variable.

Discrete-Time Signals
  - Defined only at specific, equally spaced instants of time.
  - Produced by sampling a continuous signal at regular intervals.
  - Examples: digital audio samples, stock prices recorded daily, pixel values.
  - Represented as x[n], where n is an integer index.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. BASED ON AMPLITUDE (ANALOG vs. DIGITAL)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Analog Signals
  - Amplitude can take any value within a continuous range.
  - Examples: AM/FM radio waves, audio from a microphone, voltage from a sensor.

Digital Signals
  - Amplitude is restricted to a finite set of discrete levels (usually 0 and 1).
  - Examples: computer data, digital audio, binary communication streams.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. BASED ON DETERMINISM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Deterministic Signals
  - Completely predictable at any point in time; described by a mathematical function.
  - Examples: sine waves, square waves, ramp signals.

Random (Stochastic) Signals
  - Cannot be predicted exactly; described using statistical properties.
  - Examples: thermal noise, speech, seismic activity, stock market fluctuations.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. BASED ON PERIODICITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Periodic Signals
  - Repeat themselves exactly after a fixed interval called the period (T).
  - Condition: x(t) = x(t + T) for all t.
  - Examples: sine wave, cosine wave, square wave, sawtooth wave.

Aperiodic (Non-Periodic) Signals
  - Do not repeat themselves over time.
  - Examples: a single pulse, speech signals, exponential decay.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. BASED ON DIMENSIONALITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

One-Dimensional (1D) Signals
  - Vary with a single independent variable (usually time).
  - Examples: audio signals, ECG, temperature vs. time.

Two-Dimensional (2D) Signals
  - Vary with two independent variables (e.g., spatial coordinates).
  - Examples: grayscale images (intensity as a function of x and y).

Multi-Dimensional Signals
  - Vary with three or more independent variables.
  - Examples: video (2D space + time), MRI scans (3D volumetric data).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. BASED ON ENERGY AND POWER
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Energy Signals
  - Have finite total energy and zero average power.
  - Examples: a single rectangular pulse, a decaying exponential.

Power Signals
  - Have finite average power and infinite total energy.
  - Examples: periodic signals (sine waves, square waves), random noise.

Neither Energy nor Power Signals
  - Signals that are unbounded (e.g., a ramp signal x(t) = t).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. BASED ON SYMMETRY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Even (Symmetric) Signals
  - Mirror image about the vertical axis: x(t) = x(-t).
  - Examples: cosine wave, Gaussian pulse.

Odd (Anti-Symmetric) Signals
  - Negative mirror image: x(-t) = -x(t).
  - Examples: sine wave, signum function.

Neither Even nor Odd
  - Most real-world signals fall into this category.
  - Any signal can be decomposed into an even and an odd component.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. BASED ON CAUSAL NATURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Causal Signals
  - Zero for all time before t = 0; they "start" at some point.
  - Examples: unit step function, physical system responses.

Anti-Causal Signals
  - Zero for all time after t = 0.

Non-Causal Signals
  - Exist for both negative and positive time.
  - Examples: two-sided exponential, ideal sinc function.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. BASED ON ORIGIN / DOMAIN OF APPLICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Biomedical Signals
  - Originate from the human body and carry physiological information.
  - Examples: ECG (heart), EEG (brain), EMG (muscles), blood pressure waveforms.

Communication Signals
  - Carry information over a channel.
  - Examples: modulated radio signals (AM, FM, QAM), optical signals in fiber.

Audio Signals
  - Represent sound pressure variations.
  - Examples: speech, music, sonar pulses.

Video / Image Signals
  - Represent visual information spatially and temporally.
  - Examples: video streams, satellite imagery, X-ray images.

Seismic Signals
  - Represent vibrations in the Earth's crust.
  - Examples: earthquake waveforms, oil exploration data.

Financial Signals
  - Represent economic data varying over time.
  - Examples: stock prices, exchange rates, trading volume.

Control Signals
  - Used to regulate systems and machines.
  - Examples: PID controller output, servo motor commands.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. BASED ON SPECTRAL CHARACTERISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Baseband Signals
  - Occupy a frequency range starting near 0 Hz.
  - Examples: audio (20 Hz – 20 kHz), raw sensor data.

Bandpass Signals
  - Occupy a frequency band centered around a carrier frequency above 0 Hz.
  - Examples: modulated radio signals, wireless LAN signals.

Narrowband Signals
  - Bandwidth is very small compared to the center frequency.
  - Examples: single-channel AM radio, CW radar.

Wideband / Broadband Signals
  - Spread across a large frequency range.
  - Examples: spread-spectrum communications, ultra-wideband (UWB) radar.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
11. SPECIAL / ELEMENTARY SIGNAL TYPES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Unit Impulse (Dirac Delta) — δ(t)
  - An idealized signal of infinite amplitude and zero duration with unit area.
  - Used as a mathematical tool to analyze system responses.

Unit Step — u(t)
  - Zero for t < 0, one for t ≥ 0.
  - Models switching events and is used in control analysis.

Ramp Signal
  - Linearly increases with time: x(t) = t · u(t).

Sinusoidal Signal
  - x(t) = A sin(2πft + φ); the building block of Fourier analysis.

Exponential Signal
  - x(t) = A·e^(αt); models growth (α > 0) or decay (α < 0).

Sinc Signal
  - sinc(t) = sin(πt)/(πt); fundamental to sampling theory.
